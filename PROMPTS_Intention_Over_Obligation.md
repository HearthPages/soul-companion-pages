# Intention Over Obligation — Deep Exploration Prompts

## Base System Prompt

Copy and paste this into Claude/ChatGPT first:

```
You are a warm, thoughtful companion for women working through the Intention Over Obligation workbook.

Philosophy: The shift from "I have to" to "I choose to" is one of the most profound acts of self-respect available to us. There are no right answers. Honesty matters more than positivity. Skipping a day is not failure.

As she works through the workbook, she will explore different questions. When she wants to go deeper on a specific question, she will share it with you along with her responses, and you will ask 3-5 questions to help her explore further.

Your role: Listen carefully to what she shares. Reflect back what you heard. Ask genuine follow-up questions that go deeper. Be conversational, like a trusted friend.

Tone: Warm, present, unhurried. Never clinical or performative. Speak like you're actually listening.

Length: Keep it natural — 2-4 sentences usually. No lists or structures. Just real conversation.
```

## Deep Exploration Prompts

Copy and paste these when she wants to explore a question more deeply.

### Section One · Realising

**The Hum Beneath the Day**

I'm working on "The Hum Beneath the Day." The question is: "Write down everything you said 'I have to' about in the last week — big and small. Don't filter. Don't justify. Just list."

I've done that and I want to explore this more deeply. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me look closer: which of these obligations did I actually choose, and which ones chose me — through expectation, habit, or pressure? What patterns do you notice about where the weight comes from?

---

**What Your Body Already Knows**

I'm working on "What Your Body Already Knows." The question is: "Your body registers obligation before your mind names it. Tightness across the chest. A heaviness behind the eyes. A faint, familiar dread. Think of something you do regularly from obligation. Where do you feel it in your body? Describe the sensation as honestly as you can."

I think I know where I feel the obligation in my body. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me find out and see if I'm right, and what my body is trying to tell me. What might this be protecting me from?

---

**The Resentment Map**

I'm working on "The Resentment Map." The question is: "Resentment is not a character flaw. It is information. It shows up wherever you have been giving from pressure rather than choice. Where in your life does quiet resentment live? Write about it without softening it."

I believe I have identified where my resentment lives quietly in my life. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me understand what it's really saying. Resentment is information — what is mine telling me about where I've been giving from pressure rather than choice?

---

**The Cost You Haven't Named**

I'm working on "The Cost You Haven't Named." The question is: "Living from obligation has a cost — not just in energy, but in who you stop being when you are always performing what is expected. What has living from obligation cost you? Not what you have done for others — what have you quietly given up of yourself?"

I can see what I've done for others from obligation. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me explore what I've stopped being. Not what I've given up doing — who have I become by always performing what's expected? What version of myself went quiet?

---

**What Freedom Feels Like**

I'm working on "What Freedom Feels Like." The question is: "When you imagine a day that belongs entirely to you — no performance, no pressure, nothing owed — what does it feel like? Not what you'd do. How it feels in your body."

I've tried to sit with what freedom actually feels like, not just what I'd do with it. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me explore: what does this feeling tell me about what I've been living without? What is the quiet knowing underneath — about what I actually need? What makes that feeling feel so far away?

---

### Section Two · Understanding

**What You Were Taught About Your Needs**

I'm working on "What You Were Taught About Your Needs." The question is: "Most women who live from obligation learned early that their needs were either too much, or less important than someone else's. That lesson left a mark. Growing up, what message did you receive about your own needs? Was it safe to have them? Was it safe to say so?"

I think I know what message I received about my needs growing up. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me explore: do I still believe that message? Where does it show up in my life now? What would it mean to question it?

---

**Who You Learned to Be**

I'm working on "Who You Learned to Be." The question is: "We adapt. We become the version of ourselves that felt safest — the helpful one, the quiet one, the one who never caused trouble. That adaptation made sense then. It kept you safe. But it came at a cost. Who did you learn to be in order to feel loved or safe? What did that version of you have to give up?"

I think I can see the version of myself I became to stay safe or loved. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me understand what that adaptation cost me. What parts of myself did I have to hide or suppress? What did that version give up?

---

**The Voice Behind the Should**

I'm working on "The Voice Behind the Should." The question is: "Every 'I should' carries a voice. Not always our own. Often someone else's — a parent, a teacher, a culture. Choose one 'should' that sits heavily on you right now. Whose voice is really behind it? What were they afraid of? What were you?"

I think I can hear the voice behind one of my "shoulds" that sits heavily. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me explore: what is that voice really afraid of? And underneath that fear, what am I still afraid of? Where does that fear live in my life now?

---

**A Letter to the Girl Who First Learned This**

I'm working on "A Letter to the Girl Who First Learned This." The question is: "She did what she had to do. She adapted, she smiled, she made herself smaller or more useful or easier to love. She was doing her best with what she had. She deserves to hear from you now. Write a letter to the younger version of you who first learned to put herself last."

I believe I have identified what that younger version of me learned. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me understand it with compassion. What did she need to hear? What would I tell her about who she actually was underneath the adaptation?

---

**What You Believed About Yourself**

I'm working on "What You Believed About Yourself." The question is: "Somewhere in all of this, a belief took root. Not a dramatic one — a quiet one. Something about what you deserve, what you're worth, what you have to earn before it's okay to want something for yourself. What do you believe, underneath everything, that you have to be or do in order to deserve rest? Softness? Space?"

I've named the belief I carry about what I have to earn before I deserve softness. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me examine it. Is this belief actually mine, or was it handed to me wrapped up to look like fact? What would change if I simply let myself be enough, as I am?

---

### Section Three · Releasing

**Guilt Is Not a Compass**

I'm working on "Guilt Is Not a Compass." The question is: "We treat guilt as though it is always telling us the truth — that choosing ourselves makes us selfish. Where in your life does guilt reliably show up when you try to choose yourself? What story does it tell you? Is that story actually true?"

I think I know where guilt shows up when I try to choose myself. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me understand what it's really protecting. What's underneath the guilt? What would happen if I didn't listen to it?

---

**The Chronic Yes**

I'm working on "The Chronic Yes." The question is: "Every yes that comes from fear or pressure is also a no to something — your time, your energy, your own needs. The cost is real. What have you been saying yes to that you wish you hadn't? What have those yeses cost you?"

I think I can see what I've been saying yes to from fear. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me understand the real cost. What have those yeses quietly prevented me from doing or becoming?

---

**What You've Been Protecting**

I'm working on "What You've Been Protecting." The question is: "Sometimes we stay small — agreeable, obliging, endlessly available — because it feels safer than the alternative. What is the thing you are most afraid would happen if you stopped? Sit with that fear honestly."

I believe I can name the thing I'm afraid would happen if I stopped obliging. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me look underneath it. What is that fear really about? Where did it come from?

---

**What Releasing Actually Looks Like**

I'm working on "What Releasing Actually Looks Like." The question is: "Releasing is rarely a dramatic gesture. It is usually quiet. A boundary held gently but clearly. A no that doesn't come with an apology attached. What is one obligation you are genuinely ready to release? What would releasing it look like in practice — not in theory, but in your actual life?"

I believe I have identified something I'm ready to release. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me think through what releasing it would actually look like — not in the abstract, but concretely. What do I need to believe about myself in order to actually do it?

---

**The Space Left Behind**

I'm working on "The Space Left Behind." The question is: "When we release something we've been carrying for a long time, there is often a strange feeling that follows. Not just relief. Sometimes grief. Sometimes unfamiliarity. Sometimes a quiet that feels unearned. If you let go of the obligation or pattern you named — what might the space left behind feel like? What might be possible in it?"

I've tried to imagine the space that would open if I let something go. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me sit in that space more honestly. What am I afraid might be in it? What am I quietly hoping for? What would I want to grow there?

---

### Section Four · Returning

**What You Actually Enjoy**

I'm working on "What You Actually Enjoy." The question is: "Not what you think you should enjoy. Not what looks responsible or sounds productive. What you genuinely, quietly enjoy — even if it seems too small to matter. When did you last do something purely because you wanted to? What was it?"

I've named something I genuinely enjoy. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me explore: why have I allowed myself so little of this? What story have I told myself about why it's not allowed? Is that story actually true?

---

**Desire Without the Guilt Filter**

I'm working on "Desire Without the Guilt Filter." The question is: "Desire filtered through guilt doesn't sound like desire anymore. It sounds like 'maybe someday' or 'I don't really need it.' Remove the filter for a moment. If guilt wasn't in the room, what would you want more of in your life right now?"

I can feel the desire underneath the guilt. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me understand what I'm really wanting. If guilt wasn't there, what would I choose? What does that desire tell me about what matters to me?

---

**What You Deserve**

I'm working on "What You Deserve." The question is: "This is the one most of us skip. Not because we didn't read it — but because something in us goes quiet when we reach it. What do you believe you don't deserve? Be honest, even if it feels uncomfortable. Where did that belief come from?"

I've named something I believe I don't deserve. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me sit with this more carefully. Is this belief actually mine, or did someone teach it to me? What if it's not true?

---

**What You've Been Calling Selfish**

I'm working on "What You've Been Calling Selfish." The question is: "Rest. Space. Time that belongs only to you. The word 'selfish' has done a great deal of quiet damage. What have you been calling selfish that is actually just a need? What would it feel like to call it a need instead?"

I've identified something I've been calling selfish. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me explore: is this actually selfish, or is it just a need? What would it feel like to call it what it really is? What would change?

---

**The Things You've Missed**

I'm working on "The Things You've Missed." The question is: "You have been here. You showed up. You gave. But in the giving, something of you got quieter — the parts that weren't useful, weren't needed, weren't convenient. What parts of yourself have you been missing? Not who you want to become — who you used to be, or who you sense you still are, underneath everything."

I've named parts of myself that have gone quiet. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me understand them more fully. When did they go quiet? What made them go quiet? What would it actually take to let even one part back in?

---

### Section Five · Rooting

**What Choosing Yourself Looks Like**

I'm working on "What Choosing Yourself Looks Like." The question is: "Not in grand gestures. In the small, ordinary moments of a Tuesday. Choosing yourself often looks unremarkable from the outside — and quietly radical from within. What does choosing yourself look like in your daily life right now — not as an aspiration, but as something small and actual?"

I've identified how I choose myself in small ways. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me explore: what would it look like if I did this more consistently? What small choice could I make today that would move me toward this?

---

**When Old Habits Come Back**

I'm working on "When Old Habits Come Back." The question is: "They will. Not because you failed, but because habits are grooves worn deep by years of practice. The return of an old pattern is not failure — it is information. What are the conditions that make you most likely to slip back into obligation? What are the early signs?"

I think I know the conditions that pull me back into obligation. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me prepare: what are the earliest signs I notice? What will I actually do when I see them? Not to punish myself — just to come back.

---

**Holding Intention When Life Gets Loud**

I'm working on "Holding Intention When Life Gets Loud." The question is: "Life doesn't pause while you do this work. There will be hard weeks, competing demands, moments when everything asks for more than you have. Intention needs to be simple enough to hold onto. What is the simplest, truest version of your intention — one sentence you could come back to when everything feels like too much?"

I've found my simplest intention. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me test it: does this actually hold when life gets loud? What helps me hold it? Is there a way to make it even simpler, even truer?

---

**The Life You Are Building**

I'm working on "The Life You Are Building." The question is: "Not perfect. Not finished. Just more honest, more chosen, more yours than it was when you began. Looking at where you started and where you are now — what has shifted, even quietly?"

I can see what's shifted. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me go deeper: what am I becoming through these shifts? What does a life led more by intention than obligation look like for me, in ordinary everyday terms?

---

**What You Know Now**

I'm working on "What You Know Now." The question is: "You have held something in these pages that you didn't have words for when you began. You have looked honestly at things that were easier not to look at. What do you know now that you didn't know — or couldn't let yourself know — when you opened this workbook?"

I've written what I know now that I didn't when I began. Ask me 3-5 questions to help me sit with this more fully. What shifted the most quietly? What do I most want to carry forward — not as a resolution, but as something true?
